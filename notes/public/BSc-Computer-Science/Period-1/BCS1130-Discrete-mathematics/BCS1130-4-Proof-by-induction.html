Mathematical induction theory §

Mathematical induction

Proof technique for statements of the form ∀n≥N,n∈Z (set of integers from a starting point on, e.g. natural numbers)

  • Two steps in the proof by induction:
    • Base case (proof of P(n)) → the statement holds for the first number
    • Induction step: proof of ∀n≥N(P(n)→P(n+1))
  • Process of induction
    • P(n) is true → base case
    • P(n)→P(n+1)∀n → (induction step)
    • P(n),P(n)→P(n+1)⟹P(n+1)

Induction example §

Induction example

∀n∈N(∑k+1n​k=2n(n+1)​) Base case: Proof of P(1)

  • ∑k=11​=1=21(1+1)​=1 → This holds

** Induction step**: Proof of ∀m≥1,p(m)→P(m+1)

  • p(m):∑k=1m​k=2m(m+1)​ (assumption)
  • p(m+1):∑k=1m+1​k=2(m+1)(m+2)​ (to prove)
  • ∑k=1m+1​k=(1+2+….+m+(m+1))
    • we see that (1+2+…+m)=p(m):∑k=1m​k=2m(m+1)​
  • We use our assumption
    • ∑k=1m+1​k=(1+2+….+m+(m+1))=2m(m+1)​+(m+1)
    • =(m+1)(2m​+1)=(m+1)(2m+2​)

Induction example 2 §

Induction example 2

∀n∈N:4n−1 is divisible by 3

Base case: Proof of P(1)

  • P(1)=41−1=3 → 3 is divisible by 3$

Induction step: Proof of P(x)→P(x+1)∀x∈N

  • P(x):4x−1 is divisible by 3 (assumption / hypothesis)
  • P(x+1):4x+1−1 is divisible by 3 (to prove)

Always use your assumption hypothesis!

  • P(x+1)=4x+1−1=4⋅4x−1=3⋅4x+4x−1
    • 4x−1 is divisible by 3 by assumption (3k) → 4x−1=3k
    • 3⋅4x is also a multiple of 3
  • P(x+1)=3⋅4x+4x−1=3⋅4x+3k=3(4x+k) → multiple of 3

Induction example 3

A country has two types of coins: 3 cents and 7 cents. Prove that you can make all sums of money starting from 12 cents (up to 1 cent accuracy) from these coins.

Explaining the example 2⋅7=5⋅3 Induction proof 3.loom Negation: max 1x7, max 1x3 → cannot add up to 12 or more

  1. If we have 2x3 (at least), we can go 1 cent up
  2. If we have 2x7 (at least), we can go 1 cent up
  3. We always have to have either (or both) 2x7, or 2x3

Proof by induction

  • Base case: P(12) holds → 12=3+3+3+3
  • Inductive step: P(q)→P(q+1)
    1. q has at least 2 coins of 7 or 2 coins of 3
      • r: at least 2 coins of 7
      • s: at least 2 coins of 3
      • ¬(r∨s)=¬r∧¬s is impossible, as q ≥ 1
    2. if r is true, then we can replace 3x7 by 5x3
    3. if s is true, then we can replace 2x3 by 7
    • → in both cases, we can make q + 1
